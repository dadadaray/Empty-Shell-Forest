## 2.2 Results — Climate-driven successional arrest amplifies secondary mortality risk

What makes a forest vulnerable to the Empty Shell syndrome? The syndrome is strikingly concentrated in fire-prone regions: Australia alone accounts for 88.6% of affected sites, followed by the Mediterranean Basin (43.1%) and western North America (22.5%) (Supplementary Fig. 2a). Yet this geographic pattern does not explain which local conditions tip a recovering forest into the trap.

To answer this, we trained a machine learning model — Extreme Gradient Boosting (XGBoost)~\citep{ester2022xgboost,chen2016xgboost} — on 20 environmental predictors spanning climate, topography, soil, vegetation history, and human footprint (Fig.~\ref{fig:main_figure_2} and Methods). The model proved highly effective at identifying Empty Shell sites, achieving an AUC of 0.909. But accuracy alone does not reveal mechanism. We therefore used SHAP (SHapley Additive exPlanations)~\citep{lundberg2017unified,lundberg2020local} to open the black box and measure how much each environmental factor contributed to the prediction.

Three physical factors dominated. Maximum temperature was by far the strongest driver: the hotter the post-disturbance environment, the more likely a site would follow the Empty Shell trajectory (Fig.~\ref{fig:fig2_tmmx}). Elevation and burn severity (dNBR) ranked second and third, reflecting the compounding effects of terrain and fire intensity on recovery dynamics (Fig.~\ref{fig:fig2_dNBR}). Human footprint and sand content followed closely, with topographic wetness index (TWI) rounding out the top six physical predictors (Fig.~\ref{fig:fig2_a}). Biome Type, despite its high overall SHAP ranking, proved to be merely a spatial proxy: removing it from the model preserved nearly identical performance (AUC reduction of only 0.001, Fig.~\ref{fig:ablation}), while the weight redistributed directly onto the physical drivers — particularly maximum temperature, elevation, and sand content. This confirms that the Empty Shell syndrome is a universal climate response, not a biome-specific anomaly.

SHAP dependence curves revealed the nonlinear shapes of these constraints — and they tell an ecological story. The risk of the Empty Shell surged steeply as maximum temperatures crossed into extreme ranges. Sites at moderate elevations faced disproportionately higher risk than both lowlands and high mountains, likely reflecting the intersection of productive growing conditions and intense competitive dynamics. Burn severity showed a threshold effect: beyond a critical level of fire damage~\citep{key2006landscape}, the probability of shrub-dominated arrested succession rose sharply. Human footprint — a proxy for land-use history and fragmentation — exerted a surprisingly consistent positive influence, while sandy soils amplified the effect, accelerating moisture drainage below the rooting depth of tree seedlings.

These curves collectively point to a single mechanism: climate-driven arrested succession~\citep{davis2019wildfires, coop2020wildfire,rother2016limited}. Under extreme heat, even moderate rainfall disproportionately benefits fast-growing pioneer shrubs rather than tree seedlings. These shrubs — which create the deceptive satellite greenness signal — aggressively capture light and water, especially in well-drained sandy soils where surface moisture never reaches deeper tree roots~\citep{schenk2002global, wang2012dryland}. The result is a green forest that has stopped growing.

This hidden failure carries deadly consequences. We tracked the survival of 511 high-risk Empty Shell sites using three decades of forest loss records~\citep{hansen2013high}. Despite their rapid green-up, these forests began dying again within years. Kaplan-Meier analysis showed their survival probability dropping sharply through the first post-disturbance decade — falling significantly below genuinely recovering forests (log-rank P = 9.29 $\times$ 10$^{-8}$; Fig.~\ref{fig:fig2_KM}).

To isolate whether the Empty Shell state itself predicts death — independently of the environmental conditions that created it — we built nested Cox proportional hazards models~\citep{cox1972regression}. The syndrome alone increased secondary mortality risk by 60\% (HR = 1.60, 95\% CI [1.39, 1.85], $P = 1.61 \times 10^{-10}$). We then controlled for each of the top six physical SHAP predictors one at a time: maximum temperature (HR = 1.68), elevation (HR = 1.92), burn severity (HR = 1.60), human footprint, sand content, and TWI (Fig.~\ref{fig:fig2_COX}). The Empty Shell mortality signal persisted across all individual adjustments. There was one critical exception. Controlling for sand content erased the signal entirely (HR = 1.10, $P = 0.22$). This is exactly what the ecological mechanism predicts: sandy soils are not a confounder — they are the stage on which the competitive exclusion plays out. Fast-draining soils enable shrubs to win the race for water, causing both the Empty Shell state and the elevated mortality that follows.

## 5.3 Methods — XGBoost classification and SHAP-based feature attribution

We needed a model that could do two things at once: accurately predict which forests are susceptible to the Empty Shell syndrome, and explain why in terms of observable environmental conditions. The challenge was to build a model powerful enough to capture complex ecological interactions, yet simple enough that its logic could be inspected and understood.

We chose Extreme Gradient Boosting (XGBoost)~\citep{chen2016xgboost,ester2022xgboost}, a tree-based algorithm that excels at learning non-linear relationships from tabular data. The model evaluated 20 biophysical and anthropogenic predictors (detailed in Fig.~\ref{fig:fig2_a} and Section~\ref{5.1}) against the DTW-assigned binary target: Empty Shell (1) versus all other recovery trajectories (0). With only 601 training sites — 183 of which were Empty Shell — overfitting was a real risk. We addressed this in three ways. First, we used extremely shallow trees: a maximum depth of just 2, meaning the model could learn at most two-way interactions between predictors. Second, we set a conservative learning rate of 0.05 and randomly subsampled 80\% of both training samples and features in each boosting round, injecting noise that prevents memorization. Third, we weighted the loss function 2.28:1 against the minority class, forcing the model to take the rare Empty Shell cases seriously rather than dismissing them as noise.

Evaluated under 5-fold stratified cross-validation, the constrained model achieved an AUC of $0.909 \pm 0.026$ and an Average Precision of $0.864 \pm 0.030$ — strong performance against a baseline Empty Shell prevalence of only 30.4\% (Fig.~\ref{figS2a:ROC_PR}). We then applied the trained model to all 1,393 sites, producing a continuous Empty Shell probability ($\text{Prob}_{\text{ES}} \in [0, 1]$). This probability — not a hard yes/no label — became the foundation for every subsequent analysis: the global risk map (Fig.~\ref{fig1_a}), the survival models (Section~\ref{5.4}), and the carbon deficit calculation (Section~\ref{5.5}).

To understand what the model had learned, we used SHAP (SHapley Additive exPlanations) TreeExplainer~\citep{lundberg2017unified,lundberg2020local}. SHAP is grounded in cooperative game theory~\citep{shapley1953value}: it asks, for each prediction, how much did each feature contribute compared to what the model would have predicted without it? Summing these contributions across all 601 training sites gives each feature's global importance. Formally, for each feature $f$, we computed:

\begin{equation}
\text{Importance}_f = \frac{1}{N} \sum_{i=1}^{N} |\phi_f^{(i)}|
\label{eq:shap}
\end{equation}

where $\phi_f^{(i)}$ is the SHAP value of feature $f$ for site $i$. Features with consistently large absolute SHAP values are those the model relies on most heavily.

For continuous predictors, we went beyond ranking to map the shape of each relationship. We fitted B-spline smoothing curves (5 degrees of freedom)~\citep{hastie2009elements} through the SHAP dependence scatter, with 95\% confidence envelopes. These curves reveal ecological thresholds — the temperature at which risk surges, the elevation band where vulnerability peaks — rather than just linear trends. For the categorical Biome Type variable, we computed mean SHAP values per biome with pointwise standard errors.

We also tested a skeptical hypothesis: what if the model was merely learning where Empty Shell sites are, rather than what they are? If Biome Type — essentially a geographic label — was doing the heavy lifting, then the apparent physical drivers might be spurious. We removed Biome Type and retrained on the remaining 19 continuous predictors. The result was decisive. The ablated model performed nearly identically (AUC = 0.908, $\Delta \text{AUC} = 0.001$), and the predictive weight redistributed cleanly onto the physical drivers: maximum temperature, elevation, and sand content (Fig.~\ref{fig:ablation}). The Empty Shell syndrome is not a biome effect — it is a climate and soil effect that happens to correlate with biome boundaries.

## 5.4 Methods — Survival analysis

The Empty Shell syndrome is a satellite observation — a pattern of greenness and biomass decoupling visible from space. But does it predict anything real on the ground? To answer this, we turned to the most consequential ecological outcome: whether a forest dies again.

We linked all 1,393 sites to the Hansen Global Forest Change dataset~\citep{hansen2013high}, which maps annual forest loss at 30 m resolution from 2000 onward. For each site, we extracted the earliest recorded loss year occurring strictly after the initial disturbance — that is, the first time the site lost forest cover again after supposedly recovering. Sites with a positive time-to-death were classified as secondary mortality events. Sites that survived to 2023 without further loss were right-censored, with observation windows capped at 40 years to prevent survival bias from extremely old disturbances.

We first asked a simple non-parametric question: do Empty Shell sites die faster? Splitting the 1,393 sites at $\text{Prob}_{\text{ES}} = 0.5$, we constructed Kaplan-Meier survival curves. The answer was unambiguous. The Empty Shell group (511 sites) showed sharply accelerated mortality, with survival probability diverging from the reference group (882 sites) within the first five post-disturbance years (Mantel--Cox log-rank $\chi^2 = 28.52$, $P = 9.29 \times 10^{-8}$; Fig.~\ref{fig:fig2_KM}).

But correlation is not causation. The sites that became Empty Shell also tend to be hotter, drier, and sandier. Maybe these conditions — not the Empty Shell state itself — are what kill trees. To disentangle this, we used Cox proportional hazards models~\citep{cox1972regression}, which estimate the independent contribution of each variable to mortality risk while holding others constant.

A key design choice was how to encode the Empty Shell predictor. A binary cutoff ($\text{Prob}_{\text{ES}} > 0.5$) is intuitive but discards information: a site at 0.51 is treated identically to one at 0.95, and a site at 0.49 is treated identically to one at 0.01. We instead used the continuous, Z-standardized probability, preserving the full gradient of recovery failure. This continuous specification substantially outperformed the binary version ($\Delta \text{AIC} = 13.7$~\citep{akaike1974new}, concordance index = 0.602).

In the baseline model, a one-standard-deviation increase in $\text{Prob}_{\text{ES}}$ corresponded to a 60\% increase in secondary mortality hazard (HR = 1.60, 95\% CI [1.39, 1.85], $P = 1.61 \times 10^{-10}$; Fig.~\ref{fig:fig2_COX}). But the real test came when we introduced the environmental confounders. We built nested models, each adding one of the top six physical SHAP predictors — maximum temperature, elevation, dNBR, human footprint, sand content, and TWI — alongside the Empty Shell probability. If the environmental variables fully explained the mortality signal, the Empty Shell hazard ratio should collapse toward 1.0.

It did not. Across five of six adjustments, the Empty Shell hazard remained elevated and significant: maximum temperature (HR = 1.68, $P = 2.89 \times 10^{-9}$), elevation (HR = 1.92, $P = 2.58 \times 10^{-13}$), dNBR (HR = 1.60, $P = 1.85 \times 10^{-10}$), human footprint, and TWI. The sole exception was sand content. Adding sand content to the model erased the Empty Shell mortality signal (HR = 1.10, $P = 0.22$). This is not a failure of the Empty Shell concept — it is a mechanistic confirmation. Sandy soils are the stage on which the competitive exclusion plays out: their rapid drainage favors shrubs over tree seedlings, causing both the satellite-observed decoupling and the elevated mortality that follows~\citep{davis2019wildfires, coop2020wildfire,rother2016limited}. Sand is a mediator, not a confounder.

We verified the proportional hazards assumption using Schoenfeld residual tests~\citep{schoenfeld1982partial}. Most models satisfied the assumption ($P > 0.05$), with minor violations in models adjusting for maximum VPD and dNBR, which we note. All survival analyses were performed using the \texttt{lifelines} library in Python~\citep{davidson2021camdavidsonpilon}.

Finally, we asked whether these results depend on arbitrary algorithmic choices. The Empty Shell mortality signal proved robust. It persisted across cluster counts ($K = 2$--$5$, with $K = 3$ yielding the most conservative HR = 1.60 vs. 1.91--1.92 for $K = 4$--$5$; Fig.~\ref{figS1a:k_trend}) and across truncated observation windows (HR = 1.78 at $W = 3$; Fig.~\ref{figS2a:window_hr}). A more stringent test came from spatial thinning — a standard procedure to remove the inflation of apparent precision from nearby, autocorrelated sites. Grid-based thinning at $0.5^\circ$ reduced the training set from 601 to just 147 sites. At this sample size, the unsupervised DTW clustering broke down, producing spurious cluster assignments and a non-significant HR of 0.91 ($P = 0.70$). This reflects the mathematical fragility of clustering with small samples, not the absence of a biological signal.

To prove this, we built a classifier that does not need clustering at all. We defined a site as Empty Shell based on two physical criteria visible in every recovery trajectory: declining VOD (woody biomass not recovering) and above-median NDVI slope (greenness recovering faster than average). This criterion-based definition requires no hyperparameters, no distance metrics, and no clustering algorithm. Although it agreed with the DTW labels only partially (123 vs. 183 sites; Cohen's $\kappa = 0.15$), it captured the same ecological essence: the downstream mortality signal was preserved. When we ran the identical survival pipeline on the criterion-labeled, spatially thinned data, the hazard ratio remained positive across all grid resolutions and reached statistical significance at $1.0^\circ$ (HR = 1.56, 95\% CI [1.06, 2.27], $P = 0.023$; Table S3). The Empty Shell phenomenon is real — it survives every robustness test we subjected it to.
